History of Fraktur

The 99% Invisible episode titled "Fraktur" delves into the history of the Fraktur typeface, exploring its evolution from a widely used script in medieval Europe to a symbol of German nationalism. The episode discusses how Fraktur's association with German identity intensified during the 19th century, especially after Germany's unification in 1871, when it became the official government typeface

Fraktur Basics

The Backlog Archivists and Historians blog post "Fraktur Basics" explores the history and characteristics of the Fraktur script, a blackletter typeface prevalent in Germany from the mid-1500s until 1941. It highlights Fraktur's distinctive angular strokes and its applications in genealogical research, particularly in historical German-language documents such as obituaries and church records. The article also provides resources for deciphering Fraktur, including charts and transliteration tools.

Fruktur (Font History)

The article "Fraktur (Font History)" on Graphic Design Resource delves into the origins and characteristics of the Fraktur typeface, commissioned by Emperor Maximilian I in the early 16th century. Noted for its intricate and bold style, Fraktur draws from earlier Gothic typefaces like Textura and Schwabacher. Despite its historical ties, it remains popular today in various modern designs and uses.
